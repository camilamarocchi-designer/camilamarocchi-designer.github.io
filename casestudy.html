04 — Self-initiated Case Study

OpenShelf

Trust-based school book marketplace — AI-verified condition, fair prices, no hassle.

Product Strategy UX Research UI Design AI-Driven PWA Buenos Aires

The problem

Argentine families spend hundreds on required school books every year — and the second-hand alternative is broken.

Buying everything new isn't always viable. But the second-hand alternative comes with real friction: no condition verification, no pricing reference, and coordination happening chaotically across WhatsApp groups. Families already have a workaround. It just doesn't work well enough.

No objective condition verification

Second-hand transactions rely on blurry photos and seller goodwill. There's no standard.

No pricing reference

Sellers guess. Buyers haggle. Neither party has a baseline for fair value.

Resets every year

The problem repeats for every child, every grade, every school year — without a structural fix.

Social friction

Asking publicly in WhatsApp groups exposes families. The stigma of buying used keeps many from trying at all.

The insight

Trust comes from the object, not the seller.

Trust in second-hand marketplaces usually comes from seller reputation. OpenShelf flips that: Vellum AI — the product's verification engine — analyzes photos of each book and generates an objective condition score. The seller never has to write a description. The buyer never has to guess.

Objective AI verification

Deep Learning models analyze stains, humidity, and spine wear — eliminating seller bias entirely.

School-centered structure

Organized by institution, grade, and booklist — a tool built around how families actually buy books each year.

Smart proximity

Exchanges prioritized at nearby pickup points, turning a transaction into a frictionless neighborhood experience.

Privacy by default

No names, no school affiliation visible. The transaction happens between the book and the buyer. Not between neighbors.

Key design decisions

The goal wasn't a feature-rich marketplace. It was the fewest possible steps between a parent and a book. Every unnecessary step was a reason to abandon.

No direct chat between users

Reserve = commit. Price is fixed. No negotiation, no off-platform deals. Direct contact removes the trust guarantee.

Magic link instead of password

A parent logging in once or twice a year shouldn't need to remember a password. Magic link via email eliminated that friction entirely.

School as distribution channel

The school uploads one Excel file per year and sends two emails. No moderation, no management. Reducing their role made adoption realistic.

Privacy by default

No names, no school affiliation visible. The transaction happens between the book and the buyer. Not between neighbors.

Product strategy

The school calendar isn't a constraint. It's the engine.

OpenShelf anchors to a cycle that already exists and resets itself every year. The product doesn't need to acquire new users every year — it needs to reactivate the ones it already has.

Nov – Dec

Build supply

Families list books their children no longer need. Price suggested automatically based on past local sales.

January

Activate demand

School sends its annual booklist via a single QR code. Parents land directly on their child's grade list.

Feb – Mar

Transact

Peak buying season. Browse, reserve, and coordinate pickup. No negotiation, no chat, no friction.

Apr – Oct

Dormant but retained

Users stay registered. When November arrives, the cycle restarts automatically.

Key screens

A mobile web experience — no download required.

Every screen was designed for a time-pressured parent on a phone, making decisions late at night. No onboarding tours, no empty states, no unnecessary steps.

Design Process

AI as a strategic co-pilot, not just a design tool.

I didn't use AI to generate ideas — I used it to pressure-test mine. Every major decision in OpenShelf started with my own hypothesis. Claude helped me stress-test it, catch blind spots, and move faster once direction was clear.

1 — Product pivot · Catching my own wrong user

While updating a slide, I realized school books in Argentina aren't imported — they're local. The pain point I'd built around international shipping didn't apply to my actual user. I mentioned it: "los libros escolares se compran en argentina, no se piden afuera." Claude confirmed it was a significant error — I was mixing two distinct users with two distinct problems. That single correction reframed the entire product from a general book marketplace to a school-centered exchange.

2 — Scope control · Cutting the school dashboard

Claude had proposed a coverage dashboard for schools: "28 of 45 books for 4th grade are available." It sounded useful. But I pushed back: "Para qué le sirve ver los disponibles en el mercado AL COLEGIO?" The school's job is to upload a booklist and send one email per year — that's it. The availability problem belongs to parents, not institutions. Claude acknowledged the scope creep immediately and we simplified the school role to three steps.

3 — UX flow · Designing the onboarding with no friction

I mapped the user journey step by step in conversation, questioning each moment of friction out loud. I caught that one QR per grade was operationally impossible for a school secretary — and proposed a single QR per school instead, with grade selection happening inside the app. I also suggested replacing passwords with DNI authentication. Claude refined the logic, confirmed which steps held up, and flagged the ones that didn't.

4 — UI decision · Solving pricing display on the card

I identified that showing a single price on a book card was misleading when multiple sellers offered different prices. I proposed "Desde $x.00" as the solution. Claude validated it as an established pattern used by platforms like Airbnb and Booking, and structured how it would cascade into the detail view — all sellers, their individual prices, and Vellum scores visible on tap.
